Reading

3 passages, 40 questions, 60 minutes. There is no extra time to transfer answers — write them as you go.

Passage 1 · Questions 1–13

The rediscovery of the spice trade

Long before global shipping, a network of overland and sea routes carried pepper, cinnamon and nutmeg from South and Southeast Asia to the kitchens and pharmacies of Europe. These spices were not merely flavourings. In medieval Europe they were used as medicines, as preservatives, and as visible symbols of wealth: a host who could season a feast generously was advertising both money and connections.

The economics were extraordinary. A sack of nutmeg that cost very little at its source could be sold, after months of dangerous travel, for many times its original price. Because no single trader controlled the whole route, value was added at every stage, and the people who profited most were often the middlemen who never saw the plants growing. This created a powerful incentive to find a direct sea route to the source, and that search reshaped world history.

It is sometimes assumed that spices were rare in Asia itself, but the opposite was true. In the islands where nutmeg and cloves grew, they were ordinary and inexpensive. Their astonishing European price was created almost entirely by distance, risk and the number of hands the goods passed through. Scarcity, in other words, was manufactured by geography rather than by nature.

The arrival of European ships in the sixteenth century changed the balance. Trading companies sought not just to buy spices but to control where they were grown, sometimes restricting cultivation to a few islands to keep prices high. This deliberate management of supply is an early example of a strategy still used by some industries today. It also had severe human consequences for local populations, a dimension that older histories of the trade tended to ignore.

Modern historians have revised the traditional story in two ways. First, they emphasise that Asian and African traders were sophisticated participants, not passive suppliers waiting to be discovered. Second, they note that the cultural exchange ran in both directions: alongside spices travelled techniques, words and tastes. The history of the spice trade, once told mainly as a tale of European adventure, is increasingly understood as a shared and uneven global story.

Passage 2 · Questions 14–26

How cities cool themselves

A   On a summer afternoon a city can be several degrees hotter than the countryside around it. This effect, known as the urban heat island, is caused by dark surfaces that absorb sunlight, a shortage of vegetation, and heat released by vehicles and air conditioning. As more of the world’s population moves into cities, managing urban heat has become a serious public-health question, not merely a matter of comfort.

B   One of the simplest responses is also one of the oldest: trees. A mature street tree cools its surroundings in two ways, by shading surfaces and by releasing water vapour. Studies in several cities have found measurable temperature reductions along well-planted streets. The challenge is that trees take years to mature and require maintenance, so the benefit arrives slowly and unevenly.

C   A faster intervention is to change the colour of the city. Painting roofs white, or using pale paving, reflects sunlight rather than absorbing it. Some cities have launched large-scale cool-roof programmes, and the physics is well understood. However, the effect on the air at street level is smaller than the effect on the roof itself, so reflective surfaces work best in combination with other measures.

D   Water offers another route. Fountains, ponds and even temporary sprays lower the temperature of the air immediately around them through evaporation. Designers increasingly build small water features into squares, although in regions where water is scarce this solution raises difficult trade-offs.

E   Perhaps the most promising approach is to treat the problem as a system rather than a series of fixes. Combining trees, reflective surfaces, shaded walkways and careful building design produces results greater than any single measure. Crucially, the benefits are not shared equally: poorer districts often have fewer trees and more hard surfaces, so where cooling is installed is as important as how.

Passage 3 · Questions 27–40

The science of habit

Why is it so hard to change what we do, even when we know better? Research into habit suggests that much of daily behaviour is not the product of conscious decision at all. Instead, habits are learned sequences that the brain automates to save effort. A cue — a time, a place, an emotional state — triggers a routine, which delivers a reward, and over many repetitions the link between cue and routine becomes almost automatic.

This automation is useful. If every action required deliberate thought, ordinary life would be exhausting. The same mechanism, however, explains why unwanted behaviours persist. Willpower, the popular hero of self-improvement, turns out to be a limited and unreliable resource. People who successfully change habits rarely rely on willpower alone; instead they redesign the cues and rewards around them.

One consistent finding is that it is easier to replace a habit than to erase it. Because the cue and the craving are difficult to remove, the most effective strategy is to keep the cue and the reward but insert a new routine between them. A person who snacks when stressed, for example, may find it easier to substitute a short walk than to simply resist the snack through effort.

Environment matters more than most people assume. Studies of people who move house or change jobs show that disrupted routines create a brief window in which new habits form unusually easily. This suggests that the popular emphasis on personal discipline understates the power of context. Changing where and when something happens can be more effective than trying harder.

None of this means change is simple. Habits formed over years are not rewritten in days, and progress is rarely smooth. But the research offers a more encouraging picture than the language of failure and willpower implies: behaviour is malleable, provided we work with the brain’s machinery rather than against it.

03

Writing

60 minutes. Task 1: at least 150 words in ~20 minutes. Task 2: at least 250 words in ~40 minutes. Task 2 carries more weight.

Task 1 · Academic (process)

The diagram below shows how household waste is processed at a recycling plant. Summarise the information.

Stages (described, since this is text-only): 1 mixed waste delivered by truck → 2 large items removed by hand → 3 magnets remove metal → 4 air blowers separate paper → 5 remaining glass and plastic sorted by machine → 6 sorted materials pressed into bales → 7 bales sent to manufacturers.

Band 9 model answer (Task 1)
The diagram illustrates the seven main stages by which household waste is processed at a recycling plant, from collection through to dispatch. Overall, the process is linear and combines manual and automated sorting before the materials are compacted for reuse.

At the first stage, mixed waste is delivered to the plant by truck. Workers then remove large items by hand, after which the waste passes a series of magnets that extract metal. In the following stage, powerful air blowers separate the lighter paper from the heavier material.

The remaining glass and plastic are then sorted automatically by machine. Once separated, each type of material is pressed into compact bales. In the final stage, these bales are transported to manufacturers, where they re-enter production. The process is therefore continuous, requiring relatively little human input after the initial removal of bulky objects.

Why Band 9: clear overview (linear, manual + automated); accurate sequencing with varied passive structures; sequencing language (“then”, “once”, “in the final stage”); 152 words.

Task 2 · Essay

In many countries, people are working longer hours and have less leisure time. What are the causes of this, and what effects does it have on individuals and society?

Band 9 model answer (Task 2)
In a growing number of countries, working hours are lengthening while free time shrinks. This essay will examine the principal causes of this trend before considering its consequences for both individuals and the wider society.

Several factors explain longer working hours. The most significant is economic insecurity: as the cost of housing and essentials rises faster than wages, many people simply must work more to maintain their standard of living. A second cause is technological. Smartphones and email have blurred the line between work and home, so that employees remain reachable, and effectively on duty, long after they leave the office. Finally, in some workplace cultures, visible overwork is rewarded with promotion, creating pressure to stay late regardless of output.

The effects are largely negative. For individuals, chronic overwork is strongly associated with stress, poor sleep and damaged relationships, since time for family and health is squeezed out. At the level of society, the consequences are subtler but serious. Exhausted employees are less productive and more prone to error, which can offset the very gains employers seek. Communities also weaken when people are too tired to participate in them, from volunteering to simply knowing their neighbours.

In conclusion, longer working hours stem mainly from economic pressure, constant connectivity and cultural expectations, and they tend to harm health, relationships and even productivity. Addressing the problem will require not only individual choices but changes in how workplaces define commitment.

Why Band 9: answers both questions fully; causes and effects clearly separated; specific, developed reasoning rather than lists; precise vocabulary (“economic insecurity”, “blurred the line”); 252 words.

Speaking

Topic: Work or study & free time

• Do you work or are you a student?

• What do you enjoy most about it?

• How do you usually spend your free time?

Sample answer + why it works
Q: Has the way you spend free time changed since you were a child?
Quite a lot, actually. As a kid I was almost always outdoors, playing football with whoever was around. These days my free time is much quieter — I mostly read or meet a couple of friends for coffee. I suppose I value calm more now than I did then.

Direct answer, a then/now contrast, a reflective final sentence. Natural, not memorised.

Part 2 · Long turn

Cue card

Describe a decision you made that turned out well. You should say:
• what the decision was
• when and why you made it
• how you felt at the time
and explain why it turned out to be a good decision.

1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1–2 minutes.

Model long turn + notes

Notes: decision = move to a new city for a course / when = two years ago, wanted a fresh start / felt = nervous + excited / why good = better opportunities, more confident, new friends.

The decision I’d like to talk about is moving to a different city to take a course I really wanted to do. I made it about two years ago. The main reason was that I’d felt a bit stuck where I was, and I wanted a genuine fresh start rather than just complaining about things.

At the time I felt a strange mix of nervous and excited. Nervous because I didn’t know anyone there and the course was demanding; excited because it felt like I was finally doing something instead of waiting.

Looking back, it turned out to be one of the best choices I’ve made. Professionally it opened doors I wouldn’t have had otherwise, but honestly the bigger change was personal — I became far more independent and confident, and I made a small group of friends I’m still close to. So even though it was uncomfortable at first, it’s a decision I’ve never regretted.

Covers all four bullets, uses notes loosely, natural connectors (“Looking back”, “honestly”), ~1 min 50.

How this test is scored

Listening and Reading: raw score out of 40 converts to a band (see each section’s guide). Writing and Speaking: four criteria each, equally weighted — Task Response/Fluency, Coherence, Vocabulary, Grammar. The overall band is the average of the four skills, rounded to the nearest half.
