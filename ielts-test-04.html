SECTION 4. Today’s lecture: why we sleep. We spend about a third of our lives asleep, yet for most of human history what happens during sleep was a mystery. Modern science has begun to fill that gap, and I want to walk you through what we now know. First, the basics. Adults need on average eight hours of sleep per night to function at their best; children need rather more. Sleep is divided into four main stages, which cycle several times across the night. In stage one, brain activity slows and the body relaxes — this is the brief transition into sleep, the moment we “drift off”. Stage two is longer — it makes up about half the night — and it is when memory consolidation happens; the brain replays the day and decides what to keep. Stage three is the deepest sleep, sometimes called slow-wave sleep, when the body does its tissue repair — muscle growth, immune-system maintenance, all the unglamorous biology. Finally there is REM sleep — REM stands for rapid eye movement — which makes up roughly twenty per cent of the night and is when most vivid dreaming occurs. During sleep, the body also produces growth hormone, which is essential particularly for children but also for adult repair processes. Why we yawn

Few human behaviours are as universal — or as poorly understood — as the yawn. From newborn babies to the very old, almost every person on earth yawns several times a day. So do many other animals. Yet the function of yawning, and why it appears to spread from one person to another in a room, are questions science has only recently begun to answer.

A yawn lasts on average about six seconds. During it, the jaw stretches widely, the inhalation deepens dramatically, and the eyes typically water for a moment as the muscles around them tense. It is one of the few human behaviours that, once it begins, is almost impossible to stop.

Yawning is not, as was long assumed, unique to humans or even to primates. It has been carefully observed in animals as different as fish, birds, dogs and snakes, suggesting a deep evolutionary origin. Among most of these creatures, however, the social, contagious dimension of yawning seems mostly absent; only in a few highly social species — humans, chimpanzees and a handful of others — does watching another individual yawn reliably trigger a yawn in oneself.

Why we yawn at all is still debated. The oldest popular theory — that yawning brings more oxygen into the body — has not held up well: experiments have found that breathing pure oxygen does not stop people yawning. A more promising current proposal links yawning to a need to cool the brain. The argument is that the deep inhalation of cool air, combined with the stretching of facial muscles, lowers the temperature of blood flowing to the brain by a fraction of a degree, helping it work more efficiently. Studies have found that people yawn more often when the air around them is cool than when it is hot — the opposite of what an oxygen theory would predict.

Then there is the puzzle of contagion. Watching, hearing, or even reading about a yawn makes most people yawn within minutes — an effect easily demonstrated in any classroom. The contagious effect is strongest between people who are emotionally close: between family members and good friends it is dramatic; between strangers it is far weaker. This finding has led several researchers to argue that contagious yawning is a form of empathy — a small, unconscious way of synchronising with another person.

Even when we are alone, yawning increases when we are tired, bored or under-stimulated, and it spreads more easily through groups of people who are tired. This has practical implications: a single yawn during a long meeting really can change the room. Sleep researchers note that frequent unexplained yawning during the day can also be a useful warning sign of poor sleep at night.

Passage 2 · Questions 14–27

You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–27.

The history of the umbrella

Section A   The umbrella is older than most people realise. The familiar dome of canvas or nylon on its slender spokes feels like a modern invention, an item that arrived along with the rest of the consumer world, but in fact some form of umbrella has been carried by human beings for at least four thousand years.

Section B   The earliest known umbrellas were not designed to keep off rain. They were designed to keep off the sun. In ancient Egypt, Mesopotamia and especially China, parasols were carried over the heads of rulers and high officials by attendants, and the right to be shaded in this way was reserved by law for people of high rank. The umbrella, in other words, began life as a clear symbol of status as much as a piece of practical equipment. Paintings and carvings from the period show kings, queens and nobles under sunshades; ordinary people are bareheaded.

Section C   The rain umbrella, paradoxically, is a more recent invention. In Europe the parasol had been carried by women for sun protection since the Renaissance, but using one in the rain — and worse, using one if you were a man — was considered both eccentric and unmasculine. The transformation began in mid-eighteenth-century London, where the writer and philanthropist Jonas Hanway became famous for carrying a rain umbrella in public despite the mockery this attracted. Within a generation his example had become the rule, and by the early nineteenth century every London gentleman carried an umbrella. Hanway was credited with introducing the rain umbrella to Europe.

Section D   The umbrella became an everyday object, rather than a luxury, with industrialisation. Nineteenth-century factories began to mass-produce umbrellas, replacing the heavy whalebone spokes of older designs with light steel frames, and adopting tightly woven fabric treated to repel water. The result was an umbrella that was cheaper, lighter and far more reliable. Within a few decades the umbrella appeared in countless paintings and was a frequent prop in the novels of writers such as Dickens, who used it to mark social class — the broken, mended umbrella of the poor against the silk-handled one of the rich.

Section E   The modern umbrella has not changed dramatically in basic design since then, and its weakness has not changed either: wind. Estimates suggest that well over a hundred million umbrellas are bought worldwide each year, and a very large share are lost, broken or abandoned in cafes within a few weeks. Several recent attempts to redesign the umbrella — bendable rods, inverted canopies, aerodynamic shapes — have made modest improvements, but a sudden gust on a wet street still defeats most of them. The basic umbrella that Hanway carried through London in 1750 would, surprisingly often, be roughly as useful as the one in your bag today.

Passage 3 · Questions 28–40

You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 28–40.

Three theories of how creativity works

Section A   Creativity is often spoken of as if it were a mysterious gift — something a person either has or lacks. The picture given by researchers, however, is rather different. Over the past century three influential theories have tried to explain what creativity is, how it operates, and what helps it flourish. They are not competing accounts so much as complementary ones, each shining a light on a different part of the process.

Section B   The first approach is associated with the American-Hungarian psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, who spent decades interviewing painters, scientists, athletes and chess players about what creative work feels like from the inside. His central finding was that creative people, while working at their best, repeatedly describe a particular state of mind: a complete absorption in the task in which time seems to pass without notice and self-consciousness disappears. He called this state flow. Flow, on his account, is not the source of creative ideas but the optimal condition in which they appear, and his work has had a lasting influence on how educators and managers think about creating the right environment for creative work.

Section C   A second, complementary theory comes from the American psychologist Teresa Amabile. While Csikszentmihalyi studied the experience of creativity from the inside, Amabile asked what observable factors actually predict creative output. In her three-component model, creative work depends on the joint presence of three things: skills in the relevant domain, creative-thinking skills (the habits of mind that allow a person to combine ideas in new ways), and what she calls intrinsic motivation — the desire to do the work for its own sake, rather than for reward. Her experiments showed that intrinsic motivation is essential: people working under heavy pressure for external reward consistently produced less creative work than people working out of curiosity.

Section D   A third, older theory comes from the British psychologist Graham Wallas, who as long ago as 1926 proposed that creative work moves through four stages. The first stage is preparation, in which a person gathers information and studies the problem. The second is incubation, a period when the conscious mind steps away and ideas develop unconsciously. The third is illumination, the often-sudden moment when a solution appears. And the fourth is verification, in which the solution is tested and refined. Wallas’s model is striking because of its second stage: the suggestion that some of creative work happens precisely when we are not actively trying. Many researchers since have found support for this idea — solutions to difficult problems often appear during a walk, a shower or a night’s sleep, not at the desk.

Section E   The three theories obviously emphasise different things. Csikszentmihalyi focuses on the felt experience of creative work; Amabile on the ingredients that make creative output more likely; Wallas on the temporal sequence in which creative work proceeds. They are not in conflict. A person preparing a difficult project (Wallas) may need particular skills and intrinsic motivation (Amabile) and may do their best work in a state of flow (Csikszentmihalyi). The three theories together describe creativity from three angles.

Section F   There are practical implications. If Csikszentmihalyi is right, organisations should provide long, uninterrupted blocks of time and reduce distractions. If Amabile is right, they should protect intrinsic motivation by avoiding heavy-handed incentives. If Wallas is right, deadlines should leave room for incubation — for the unconscious work that may not look like work. None of this is mysterious. It is simply the result of taking creativity seriously enough to study how it actually happens.

Writing

Task 1 · Academic (process diagram)

The diagram below shows the main stages in the production of chocolate from cocoa beans to finished chocolate bars. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

Write at least 150 words.  Spend about 20 minutes on this task.

Band 9 model answer (Task 1)
The diagram illustrates the six main stages by which chocolate is produced, from raw cocoa beans on the tree to the finished bars on a supermarket shelf.

Overall, the process is linear and one-directional, and it falls into two clear halves. The first three stages take place outdoors on the farm and involve natural processes, while the final three are industrial steps carried out in a factory.

At the first stage, ripe cocoa beans are harvested from cocoa trees. The harvested beans are then placed in wooden boxes and left to ferment for approximately five days, a step that develops their distinctive flavour. Once fermentation is complete, the beans are spread out and dried in the sun.

The dried beans are then transported to a factory and roasted in industrial ovens, which deepens both the colour and the aroma. After roasting, the beans are crushed and ground into a thick paste known as chocolate liquor. In the final stage, the paste is mixed with sugar and milk, refined and tempered, and finally poured into moulds where it sets into the familiar chocolate bars sold in shops.

The process therefore moves the same raw material through six tightly linked steps, requiring relatively little intervention until the final, industrial stages.

Why this is Band 9: clear overview (linear; natural then industrial halves); accurate sequencing with varied passive structures and sequencers (“once”, “then”, “in the final stage”); approx. 210 words.

Task 2 · Essay (two-part question)

More and more people are choosing to live alone. What are the reasons for this trend, and is it a positive or negative development?

Write at least 250 words.  Spend about 40 minutes on this task.

Band 9 model answer (Task 2)
In many countries a growing share of households now consists of just one person. This essay will examine the principal reasons behind that trend before considering whether, on balance, it is a positive or negative development. In my view, although solo living brings real benefits to individuals, its negative social consequences are at least as serious.

Several factors explain the rise. Economically, more people can now afford to live alone: wages in many service economies have risen enough that small flats, particularly in cities, are within reach of single earners in a way that was unthinkable a generation ago. Demographically, populations are ageing, and older widows and widowers increasingly choose, or are obliged, to live by themselves rather than move in with family. Socially, the average age of marriage has risen sharply, and a growing minority of adults choose not to marry at all, so years that earlier generations would have spent in shared households are now spent alone.

The personal advantages are real. Living alone offers privacy, freedom and a degree of control over one’s environment that shared living rarely matches. For people in the middle of their careers, or recovering from a difficult relationship, that independence can be genuinely valuable.

The disadvantages, however, are largely social rather than individual. Researchers consistently note higher rates of loneliness, mental-health difficulties and physical inactivity among long-term solo dwellers, particularly the elderly. At the community level, neighbourhoods of one-person households tend to be quieter but less neighbourly, with fewer of the everyday encounters that build local trust.

On balance, the trend reflects a real expansion of personal choice but also a quiet weakening of the social bonds that traditionally supported daily life. Both sides need to be taken seriously.

Why this is Band 9: both parts of the question fully answered; reasons and evaluation clearly separated; specific reasoning with examples; precise language (“quiet weakening”, “everyday encounters that build local trust”); approx. 305 words.

Honestly, autumn — without much hesitation. The light is gorgeous in October especially, the streets aren’t as crowded as in summer, and the temperature is just about perfect for walking everywhere, which is how I most like getting around. The place I’d like to describe is the small reading room at the public library a few streets from where I live. Most people don’t even realise it’s there — you have to go through the main reading hall and turn left into what looks like a corridor, and it opens out into a long, narrow room with desk lamps and old wooden tables.

How to reach Band 7+ in Part 3

1 Use the pattern opinion → reason → example every time.

2 Show range with phrases like “it largely depends on”, “broadly speaking”, “there’s a strong argument that…”

3 Acknowledge both sides before you conclude.

4 Buy thinking time naturally: “That’s an interesting question — I’d say…”

How this test is scored

Listening and Reading: raw score out of 40 converts to a band (see each section’s guide). Writing and Speaking: four equally weighted criteria each — Task Response / Fluency, Coherence & Cohesion, Lexical Resource, and Grammatical Range & Accuracy. The overall band is the average of the four skills, rounded to the nearest half band.
